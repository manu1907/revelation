# Source Selection

## Greek Text

Working default: NA28/UBS5.

Rationale:

- The Nestle-Aland 28th edition is the standard scholarly edition of the Greek New Testament.
- UBS5 has the same Greek text as NA28, differing mainly in apparatus, punctuation, paragraphing, capitalization, and related presentation details.
- NA28 is better as the citation base for a technical exegetical argument.
- UBS5 is useful for translators and students because its apparatus is more selective and translation-oriented.

Decision:

- Use NA28 as the primary cited Greek text unless a later decision requires UBS5 presentation.
- Record any NA28/UBS5 punctuation or paragraphing issue that affects Revelation 1:1-3.
- Check whether forthcoming UBS6/NA29 developments affect Revelation 1:1-3 before final manuscript preparation.

## English Text

Working default: NIV.

Rationale:

- The NIV is widely described as the most widely read contemporary English Bible translation.
- For a US-facing general audience, the NIV is a natural representative of mainstream modern English Bible reading.
- For sales and reception, ECPA bestseller lists should be consulted by year and month because rankings can shift.

Decision:

- Use NIV as the main English comparison text for the reader-facing surface translation.
- Do not let NIV alone define "mainstream." Use a translation comparison set.

## Translation Comparison Set

Core comparison set:

- NIV: mainstream contemporary evangelical/general readership.
- ESV: mainstream formal-equivalence evangelical readership.
- KJV: historically dominant English Bible; important for inherited phrasing and public memory.
- NKJV: KJV tradition in modernized English.
- NRSVue: academic/mainline/ecumenical representative.
- NASB: formal-equivalence conservative study representative.
- CSB: contemporary evangelical balanced translation.
- NLT: dynamic/readability-oriented mainstream translation.
- NET: useful because its notes often expose translation decisions.

Possible additions:

- RSV, because of its academic and historical influence.
- NABRE, if Catholic reception becomes relevant.
- LSB or LEB, only if useful for highly literal comparison.

## Commentary Representatives

The project cannot claim to have read or represented all commentaries. It should build a representative corpus by category.

Selection criteria:

- Technical depth on the Greek text.
- Influence in academic or seminary use.
- Representation of different interpretive traditions.
- Explicit handling of Revelation 1:1-3.
- Use in later works or repeated recommendation in academic/commentary guides.
- Availability for checking exact wording and citations.

Initial representative corpus:

- David E. Aune, Word Biblical Commentary: historically critical, detailed, philological, source-critical.
- G. K. Beale, New International Greek Testament Commentary: Greek-focused, intertextual, evangelical, theological.
- Craig R. Koester, Anchor Yale Bible: academic historical-literary, reception-aware, major recent reference.
- Grant R. Osborne, Baker Exegetical Commentary on the New Testament: evangelical exegetical-pastoral, widely used.
- Robert H. Mounce, New International Commentary on the New Testament: older but influential evangelical representative.
- Richard Bauckham, not a verse-by-verse commentary but important for Revelation's structure, theology, and symbolism.
- R. H. Charles or Henry Barclay Swete: older critical representatives when reception history of interpretation matters.

Secondary representatives to consider:

- Ian Boxall, Black's New Testament Commentary.
- Stephen S. Smalley, The Revelation to John.
- Ben Witherington III, socio-rhetorical representative.
- Robert L. Thomas, dispensational/futurist technical representative.
- Craig S. Keener, accessible background-oriented representative.
- Gordon K. Beale and Sean M. McDonough on Revelation in the Commentary on the New Testament Use of the Old Testament, for intertextual handling.

## What Counts As "Mainstream"

For this book, "mainstream" should not mean unanimous, correct, or exhaustive. It means interpretations that appear repeatedly in:

- major English translations,
- widely used technical commentaries,
- seminary and pastoral commentary traditions,
- Bible-study and preaching resources,
- translation notes and study Bible notes.

An interpretation is mainstream when it is common enough that readers encounter it as the default without being told an interpretive decision has been made.

## Non-Mainstream But Relevant

Some minority readings still matter because they expose the available options:

- `en tachei` as "speedily" or manner rather than "soon."
- `apokalypsis iesou christou` as objective/content-related, "about Jesus Christ."
- Alternative referents for `auto`.
- Nonlinear readings of the communication chain.

These should be treated as control cases: they show that the Greek permits more than the default, but they do not by themselves provide the system that validates one reading and excludes another.

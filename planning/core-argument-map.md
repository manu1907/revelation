# Core Argument Map

## Purpose

This file is the control document for the book's central argument. It preserves the theory before chapters are drafted, so chapter prose can be checked against the system rather than reconstructed from memory.

## Surface, Elliptical, And Reduced Form

The book uses three related terms for the same textual phenomenon, with different emphasis:

- Surface form: the form of Revelation 1:1-3 as it appears in the Greek editions.
- Elliptical form: the same form considered as containing omitted dependents.
- Reduced form: the same form considered as the compact form from which the expanded form must be reconstructed.

The reduced form is not a translation artifact. It is the form present in the Greek editions.

## Expanded, Non-Elliptical, And Underlying Form

The book uses three related contrast terms:

- Expanded form: the form with omitted dependents supplied.
- Non-elliptical form: the form considered as no longer omitting required dependents.
- Underlying form: the form considered as the structural relation beneath the surface arrangement.

The expanded form is not a paraphrase. It is an analytical reconstruction governed by the rule-ordered system.

## Core And Non-Core Verbs

Working definition:

Core verbs are verbal heads, including one verbal noun, that participate in forming the main non-modifying clauses or clause-like units of Revelation 1:1-3.

Core clauses are the clauses or clause-like units headed by those core verbs.

Non-core verbs are verbal heads that form modifying clauses rather than the main clause-like units. The key non-core example is `dei` in `ha dei genesthai`, because it heads a modifying clause. `genesthai` is also non-core in this analysis because it is intransitive and follows `dei`.

## Seven Core Verbal Heads

1. `apokalypsis`
2. `edoken`
3. `deixai`
4. `esemanen`
5. `aposteilas`
6. `emartyresen`
7. `eiden`

Analytical notes:

- `apokalypsis` is treated as a special non-finite or gerund-like verbal head. This needs careful documentation.
- `eiden` is extranuclear because it belongs to reception rather than transmission.
- `dei` and `genesthai` are excluded from the core list.

## Lexical-Level Pattern

All seven core verbal heads denote occurrences.

Most core verbal heads are transitive and not flanked by an auxiliary. This is a key feature of the core set.

The core heads divide into transmission and reception:

- Transmission: `apokalypsis`, `edoken`, `deixai`, `esemanen`, `aposteilas`, `emartyresen`.
- Reception: `eiden`.

Within transmission:

- Transfer: `edoken`, `aposteilas`.
- Communication: `apokalypsis`, `deixai`, `esemanen`, `emartyresen`.

Within communication:

- Consciousness: `deixai`, `esemanen`.
- Truth claims: `apokalypsis`, `emartyresen`.

This lexical structure yields the chiastic sequence:

```text
A   apokalypsis   communication / truth claim
B   edoken        transfer
C   deixai        communication / consciousness
C'  esemanen      communication / consciousness
B'  aposteilas    transfer
A'  emartyresen   communication / truth claim
```

`eiden` remains outside the chiastic nucleus as an extranuclear reception element.

## Phrasal-Level Chiasm

The lexical chiasm percolates upward into the phrases headed by the core verbs.

```text
A   apokalypsis
B   iesou christou hen edoken auto ho theos
C   deixai tois doulois autou ha dei genesthai en tachei
C'  kai esemanen
B'  aposteilas dia tou angelou autou to doulo autou ioanne
A'  hos emartyresen ton logon tou theou kai ten martyrian iesou christou
```

Greek form:

```text
A   ἀποκάλυψις
B   ἰησοῦ χριστοῦ ἣν ἔδωκεν αὐτῷ ὁ θεός
C   δεῖξαι τοῖς δούλοις αὐτοῦ ἃ δεῖ γενέσθαι ἐν τάχει
C'  καὶ ἐσήμανεν
B'  ἀποστείλας διὰ τοῦ ἀγγέλου αὐτοῦ τῷ δούλῳ αὐτοῦ ἰωάννῃ
A'  ὃς ἐμαρτύρησεν τὸν λόγον τοῦ θεοῦ καὶ τὴν μαρτυρίαν ἰησοῦ χριστοῦ
```

## Rule From Chiastic Symmetry

Each element of a chiastic pair can supply missing dependents to the other element.

Example:

- `deixai` and `esemanen` share the same subject and objects.
- Therefore `esemanen`, one of the most elliptical phrases, receives subject and object dependents from `deixai`.

This must be stated as a rule, tested on each pair, and limited by clear constraints.

## Antithesis, Complementarity, And Chronology

Chiasm is inverted parallelism, so paired elements are not merely similar. Their relation can be:

- antithetical,
- complementary,
- contrastive,
- sequential,
- otherwise structurally corresponding.

Example: `edoken` and `aposteilas`.

- They are both transfer verbs.
- They contrast direct and indirect relation to the recipient.
- They involve synecdoche: God sent but also gave, which backgrounds the intermediary.
- Their chronology is not the surface order; sending precedes giving.

Because the structure is symmetrical, the same kinds of questions apply to:

- `deixai` and `esemanen`,
- `apokalypsis` and `emartyresen`.

## Transmission And Reception Model

The verses are organized by a transmission/reception model.

Transmission terms include giving, showing, signifying, sending, disclosing, and testifying.

Reception is represented by `eiden` and must be integrated as an extranuclear element.

This model matters because it requires evidence that the transmission is actually received:

- The recipient takes what is given.
- The servants become conscious of what is shown and signified.
- Truth claims are believed.

## Master-Servant Frame

`doulos` presupposes a `kyrios`, producing a master-servant or monarch-subject frame.

The frame includes:

- the master/monarch commands,
- the servant/subject receives,
- the servant/subject obeys.

This frame must be demonstrated from the text rather than imposed on it.

## John's Testimony And The Final Clause

The final clause is not secondary. The book's system must explain how John's testimony relates to:

- `ton logon tou theou`,
- `ten martyrian iesou christou`,
- `hosa eiden` or `hosa te eiden`,
- what John heard,
- what John saw.

The working claim is that John testified to what he heard and saw.

Research issue:

- NA28/UBS5-type texts appear to read `hosa eiden`, without `te`.
- Textus Receptus-type texts read `hosa te eiden`, with `te`.

This must be handled explicitly because the presence or absence of `te` affects how the final phrase is coordinated with the preceding objects of testimony. The structural claim cannot depend on silently importing a TR reading into an NA28-based argument.

Questions to answer:

- Does `hosa eiden` summarize the word of God and testimony of Jesus Christ?
- Does it stand in apposition to both preceding objects?
- Does it add a visual reception component to heard testimony?
- Does `ton logon tou theou` imply what John heard?
- Does `ten martyrian iesou christou` imply what John heard, what John saw, or both?
- How does `eiden` function as the extranuclear reception element if the surface text names only seeing?

## Immediate Interpretive Consequences

The current system yields these conclusions:

- `auto` refers to John.
- The angel is Jesus Christ.
- God is the giver and signifier.
- The things that must take place are both shown and signified.
- John testified to the word of God, the testimony of Jesus Christ, and what he saw; the relation to what he heard must be argued through `ton logon tou theou`, `ten martyrian iesou christou`, the final clause, and the presence/absence of `te`.
- The sequence of events is not linear.
- Sending precedes giving.
- Showing and signifying have a chronological relation to be defined.
- `apokalypsis` and `emartyresen` have a chronological relation to be defined.

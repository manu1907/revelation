# Progressive Argument Flow

## Purpose

The reader should feel that the book is following a process. Each chapter should solve one problem and create the need for the next step.

This file controls that progression.

## Preface: Genesis Of The Book

Function:

- Tell how the project began.
- Show the move from dissatisfaction with familiar explanations to direct examination of the Greek.
- Introduce the discovery that many mainstream readings are plausible but not sufficiently governed.
- Prepare the reader for a process of testing rather than a quick announcement of conclusions.

Should include:

- dissatisfaction with the familiar reading of `auto`,
- dissatisfaction with "servants" as Christians generally,
- dissatisfaction with `en tachei` as simply "soon,"
- the turn to Greek,
- the discovery of ambiguity in `apokalypsis iesou christou`,
- the discovery that `esemanen` and `aposteilas` require supplied constituents,
- the need for a rule-ordered system.

Should not include:

- the full expanded form,
- the complete chiasm argument,
- a dense source survey.

## Part I: Why A System Is Needed

### Chapter 1

Problem solved:

- The reader sees that Revelation 1:1-3 controls the whole reading of the book.

Question created:

- If so many things depend on these opening decisions, how are those decisions normally made?

### Chapter 2

Problem solved:

- The reader sees that several mainstream readings are plausible but underdetermined.

Question created:

- What would count as a non-arbitrary way to choose among plausible readings?

### Chapter 3

Problem solved:

- The reader receives criteria for an interpretation system: rule-ordering, predictability, consistency, economy, and exclusion.

Question created:

- What does the Greek surface form look like when read with those criteria in mind?

## Part II: How The System Is Built

### Chapter 4

Problem solved:

- The reader sees the reduced/surface form and where translation smooths it.

Question created:

- Which parts of the surface form should count as structurally central?

### Chapter 5

Problem solved:

- The reader sees the seven core verbal heads and why `dei genesthai` is treated separately.

Question created:

- Are these core heads merely a list, or do they form a structure?

### Chapter 6

Problem solved:

- The reader sees ellipsis as evidence and understands why supplied constituents must be governed.

Question created:

- Does the passage itself provide a structure that governs what can be supplied?

### Chapter 7

Problem solved:

- The reader sees the chiastic nucleus and the rule for reinstating missing dependents.

Question created:

- What does the passage look like when those dependents are reinstated?

### Chapter 8

Problem solved:

- The reader finally sees the full expanded form.

Question created:

- If this expanded form is correct, what changes in meaning follow?

## Part III: What Changes

### Chapter 9

Problem solved:

- The reader sees why "revelation" fails as the controlling rendering of `apokalypsis`.

Question created:

- If the opening term is misread, who is receiving what from whom?

### Chapter 10

Problem solved:

- The reader sees why `auto` does not refer to Jesus Christ and how agency changes.

Question created:

- If the recipient and agency shift, who are the servants?

### Chapter 11

Problem solved:

- The reader sees why "servants" cannot simply mean all Christians generally.

Question created:

- If the audience changes, what does that do to the things that must happen `en tachei`?

### Chapter 12

Problem solved:

- The reader sees why `en tachei` must be interpreted by the system rather than inherited as "soon."

Question created:

- If the things are not only soon-future events, how are they communicated?

### Chapter 13

Problem solved:

- The reader sees that the things that must take place are both shown and signified.

Question created:

- If the content is shown and signified, what exactly is John's testimony?

### Chapter 14

Problem solved:

- The reader sees John's testimony in relation to the word of God, testimony of Jesus Christ, and what John heard and saw.

Question created:

- If the opening reframes the book, how should Revelation as a whole be read?

## Part IV: How Revelation Is Reframed

### Chapter 15

Problem solved:

- The reader sees the broader interpretive frame: futurity, novelty, sequence, and multiple interpretations are all affected.

Question created:

- What are the strongest objections to this system?

### Chapter 16

Problem solved:

- The reader sees the limits, objections, and falsifiability of the proposal.

Question created:

- What remains after the objections are answered?

### Chapter 17

Problem solved:

- The reader receives the final summary and is invited to reread Revelation through the opening's constraints.

## Drafting Rule

Each chapter should end by making the next chapter feel necessary. Avoid ending chapters as if the subject is complete; end with the unresolved pressure that the next chapter takes up.

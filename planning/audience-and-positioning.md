# Audience And Positioning

## Purpose

This file turns the broad audience statement into drafting guidance. The book must be readable by serious non-specialists while remaining precise enough for pastors, seminarians, translators, commentators, and scholars to test the argument.

## Primary Reader

The primary reader is a serious general reader of Revelation.

Likely traits:

- Reads English Bible translations carefully.
- Has encountered conflicting interpretations of Revelation.
- Does not read Greek fluently.
- Is willing to learn Greek terms when they solve a real interpretive problem.
- Wants a reasoned argument, not a devotional overview or end-times chart.

What this reader needs:

- Greek shown in small portions.
- Greek script with transliteration and a short gloss at first technical use in each chapter.
- Transliteration afterward unless the Greek script itself matters.
- Literal glosses when the argument depends on word order, syntax, or lexical contrast.
- Diagrams for structure.
- Clear distinctions between "possible," "mainstream," "argued here," and "excluded by the system."

## Secondary Readers

### Pastors And Teachers

Need:

- Enough technical detail to teach responsibly.
- Clear warnings about where standard translations make interpretive decisions.
- Main-text prose that can be followed without checking every footnote.

Risk:

- They may resist the argument if it sounds like novelty for novelty's sake.

Drafting response:

- Keep the critique fair.
- Show why the mainstream reading is plausible before showing why the proposed system excludes it.

### Seminarians And Translators

Need:

- Explicit handling of Greek grammar, genitives, ellipsis, participles, infinitives, and textual variation.
- Footnotes and appendices that identify the grammar and lexicon support.
- Clear evidence tables.

Risk:

- They will notice if technical terms are used loosely.

Drafting response:

- Define project-specific terms such as "core verbal head."
- Do not let a technical term carry more weight than the evidence permits.

### Commentators And Scholars

Need:

- Page-level engagement with representative sources.
- A falsifiable method.
- Clear account of how the proposed chiasm was identified.
- A distinction between original claims and claims already supported by Greek grammar or prior scholarship.

Risk:

- They may dismiss the system if the book sounds under-sourced or overconfident where the evidence is incomplete.

Drafting response:

- Be assertive about conclusions that the system reaches.
- Be transparent about which parts are original, which are documented, and which require further verification.
- Keep named engagement mainly in footnotes, with the main text focused on interpretive patterns and the system's reasoning.

## Positioning Statement

Working version:

This book is a close reading of Revelation 1:1-3 that argues the opening is a compressed, elliptical structure rather than a simple linear communication chain. It shows how a rule-ordered analysis of the Greek changes the referents, agents, objects, timing, and relation between John's book and the entity usually called "the revelation."

## What The Book Is Not

- Not a full commentary on Revelation.
- Not a prediction scheme.
- Not a book arguing merely that mainstream readings are possible or impossible.
- Not a beginner's Greek textbook.
- Not a polemic against experts as a class.

## What The Book Is

- A theory-driven reading of Revelation 1:1-3.
- A critique of interpretation without an explicit interpretation system.
- A study of how grammar, ellipsis, and structure govern meaning.
- A proposal that the opening verses teach readers how to read the book that follows.

## Main-Text Technical Load

Default rule:

- Put only the technical detail needed for the next interpretive step in the main text.
- Put fuller grammar, source debates, and alternative analyses in footnotes or appendices.

Main text may include:

- Greek words.
- Transliteration.
- Literal glosses.
- Simple diagrams.
- Short definitions.

Main text should usually avoid:

- Long strings of untranslated Greek.
- Dense source surveys.
- Naming commentators as targets of critique.
- Extended grammar debates.
- Multiple technical labels where one explanation will do.

## Open Author Decisions

These decisions need the author's preference:

- Should the expanded form appear early as a preview of the thesis, or only after Chapters 5-7 establish the system?
- Should every Greek term be transliterated throughout, or only at first appearance in each chapter?
- How direct should the main text be when naming commentators who make mainstream decisions?
- Should the book's public-facing subtitle emphasize Revelation, Greek, or interpretation method?
- Should "disclosure" remain the working English rendering for `apokalypsis`, or should the book leave the replacement term unsettled until Chapter 9?

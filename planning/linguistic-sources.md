# Linguistic And Greek Source Plan

## Purpose

This file identifies the sources needed for the book's linguistic claims. The goal is not to bury the reader in technical literature, but to make sure every technical claim can be documented when it enters the manuscript.

## Claims Needing Support

### Genitive Ambiguity

Needed for:

- `apokalypsis iesou christou`,
- `ten martyrian iesou christou`,
- the distinction between grammatical possibility and interpretive decision.

Likely source types:

- NT Greek grammars on genitive categories.
- Commentaries on Revelation 1:1-2.
- Lexicons only where they clarify lexical range, not where the issue is syntax.

Working sources:

- Wallace, `Greek Grammar Beyond the Basics`.
- BDF, `A Greek Grammar of the New Testament and Other Early Christian Literature`.
- Smyth, `Greek Grammar`, for wider Greek control.

### Verbal Nouns And `apokalypsis`

Needed for:

- treating `apokalypsis` as a verbal noun or gerund-like head,
- distinguishing noun form from verbal force,
- explaining why the A element can participate in the verbal-head pattern.

Likely source types:

- Greek grammars on abstract nouns, verbal nouns, nominalization, and action nouns.
- Lexicons on `apokalypsis` and related verbs.
- General linguistics on nominalization if Greek grammars are too thin.

Working sources:

- BDAG.
- LSJ.
- Wallace.
- BDF.
- Huddleston and Pullum, `The Cambridge Grammar of the English Language`, only for general linguistic terminology if needed.

### Ellipsis

Needed for:

- the claim that the surface form is elliptical,
- the reconstruction of missing dependents,
- the distinction between licensed ellipsis and arbitrary supplementation.

Likely source types:

- Greek grammars on ellipsis.
- General linguistics on ellipsis and recoverability.
- Commentaries where they explicitly supply missing subjects or objects, even if they do not call this ellipsis.

Working sources:

- BDF.
- Wallace.
- Smyth.
- Huddleston and Pullum for general principles.

### Clause Heads And Core / Non-Core Distinction

Needed for:

- defining core and non-core verbal heads,
- explaining why `dei` and `genesthai` are not counted as core,
- explaining why `ha dei genesthai` is modifying.

Likely source types:

- Greek grammars on infinitive clauses and relative clauses.
- Linguistic works on heads, dependencies, and clause structure.

Working sources:

- Wallace.
- BDF.
- Porter, `Idioms of the Greek New Testament`.
- Runge, `Discourse Grammar of the Greek New Testament`.

### Lexical Relations

Needed for:

- grouping the seven heads as occurrence terms,
- distinguishing transmission from reception,
- distinguishing transfer, communication, consciousness, and truth-claim verbs,
- using terms such as hyponym.

Likely source types:

- Lexical semantics.
- Lexicons for each Greek term.

Working sources:

- Cruse, `Lexical Semantics`.
- BDAG.
- LSJ.
- Louw and Nida, `Greek-English Lexicon of the New Testament Based on Semantic Domains`.

### Chiasm And Inverted Parallelism

Needed for:

- defining chiasm at the lexical level,
- explaining how lexical correspondence can percolate to phrasal correspondence,
- preventing the chiasm from becoming a free-form pattern hunt,
- distinguishing symmetry, antithesis, complementarity, and sequence.

Likely source types:

- Studies of chiasm in biblical literature.
- General work on parallelism and discourse structure.

Working sources:

- Lund, `Chiasmus in the New Testament`.
- Welch, `Chiasmus in Antiquity`.
- Thomson, `Chiasmus in the Pauline Letters`, if needed for method.

## Page-Level Workflow

Do not cite any of these sources in a chapter until:

1. the source has a bibliography entry in `src/main.bib`;
2. the exact page or section has been checked;
3. the finding is recorded in `planning/page-reference-ledger.md`;
4. the claim is phrased at the right level of certainty.

## Drafting Principle

Technical terms should be introduced only when they do real work. When a linguistic concept can be explained in plain English without loss, use the plain-English explanation first and keep the technical term in apposition or in a footnote.

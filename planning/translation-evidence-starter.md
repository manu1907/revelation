## Early Observations

- The mainstream modern translations checked so far tend to imply that `auto` refers to Jesus Christ.
- ESV and NIV both make the linear communication chain easy to read.
- KJV/NKJV preserve `signified`, which is important for the book's argument, but they also reflect the TR `te` reading.
- NIV differs from ESV by making the opening genitive less ambiguous in English: "from Jesus Christ."
- NIV also makes the final clause read as "everything he saw," then identifies that with the word of God and testimony of Jesus Christ. That is important because it reverses the order of explanation compared with the Greek surface form.
- NRSVue and RSV preserve the old "even to all that he saw" relation, which is useful because it keeps the final clause visibly attached to the preceding testimony objects.
- NASB keeps a stronger communication term than most modern translations by using "communicated," but it still reads `auto`, `deixai`, and the chain in the mainstream way.
- CSB is close to NRSVue/ESV on the final clause but uses "whatever he saw," which leaves the appositional relation less explicit.
- NLT and NET are especially important because they expose translation decisions in opposite ways: NLT paraphrases heavily for readability, while NET gives extensive notes that name several Greek options.
- NABRE's reporting construction makes the final clause instrumental or explanatory in English, which should be compared with NIV and NLT.

## Questions Raised By The Starter Table

- Does the NIV's appositional treatment of verse 2 help or harm the book's claim about what John testified to?
- Does the ESV's "even to all that he saw" preserve the Greek structure better than NIV's rearrangement?
- Does KJV/NKJV preservation of `signified` make them useful despite their TR base?
- How do modern translations based on critical texts compensate for the absence of `te`, if at all?
- Which translations mention the textual issue in notes?
- Should NASB's "communicated" be treated as preserving `esemanen` better than "made known," or does it still flatten the signifying force?
- Does NET's supplied "then" show that even a heavily annotated translation imposes linear chronology where the Greek surface form does not state it?

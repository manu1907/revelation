# Book Bible

## Working Project

This book is a close, theory-driven reading of Revelation 1:1-3. It argues that the opening verses have been interpreted through a reduced, elliptical form that translations and commentaries commonly smooth out, while the Greek contains an expanded, non-elliptical form whose structure changes how the passage must be read.

## Working Thesis

Revelation 1:1-3 is not a simple linear statement in which Jesus Christ receives a revelation from God, shows it to Christians generally, and announces future events that must happen soon. The verses contain a compact and elliptical sequence of phrases whose internal arrangement, especially a chiastic pattern among six core phrases, allows ellipted constituents to be reinstated and compared. When this structure is attended to, several familiar interpretive decisions are excluded.

The book argues that:

- The grammatical plausibility of `apokalypsis iesou christou` as "disclosure about Jesus Christ" is a fact supported by Greek grammar and by many authors. The book's argument is not that this is merely possible, but that competing plausible options require an interpretation system that chooses among them without arbitrariness.
- Translating `apokalypsis` as "revelation" is misleading because the system excludes that meaning. "Revelation" entails that the thing revealed was previously hidden, unknown, or concealed, while the chiasm compares and contrasts `apokalypsis` with `emartyresen`; neither term requires previously hidden, unknown, or concealed content, and one does the contrary of what the other does.
- `auto` in `hen edoken auto ho theos` does not refer to Jesus Christ.
- God, not Jesus Christ, is the shower/signifier in the key sequence.
- The things that must take place are not merely shown; they are also signified.
- `en tachei` is not "soon" in the usual interpretive sense; "speedily" and other possibilities show why the phrase requires rule-ordered interpretation rather than inherited preference.
- The servants are not all Christians generally.
- The sequence of events in Revelation 1:1-3 is not linear.
- What is often called "the revelation" is not the book itself, but another entity that contrasts with the book.
- John's book is his testimony to what he heard and saw.
- Multiple interpretations are not simply accidents of reader confusion; they are related to the nature of John's visions and to the linguistic compression of the opening.

## Audience

Primary audience: serious general readers of Revelation who are willing to follow Greek-based argument when the Greek is clearly explained.

Secondary audience: pastors, teachers, seminarians, translators, commentators, and scholars interested in how Revelation 1:1-3 has been interpreted and how its grammar constrains or destabilizes standard readings.

The book should not assume the reader can read Greek fluently. It may use Greek words, transliteration, literal glosses, diagrams, and linguistic concepts, but each technical move must be introduced as a tool for solving a concrete interpretive problem.

## Positioning

This is not merely a book about "what Revelation means." It is a book about how Revelation 1:1-3 instructs readers to interpret what follows, and about how early interpretive decisions in the first three verses shape the entire book.

The tone should be rigorous but not combative. The book challenges majority interpretations while distinguishing:

- what the Greek certainly requires,
- what the Greek permits,
- what the interpretation system validates,
- what the interpretation system excludes.

## Reader Promise

By the end, the reader should understand why familiar translations of Revelation 1:1-3 are interpretive decisions, not neutral renderings, and should be able to see how a rule-ordered analysis of the Greek produces a different reading of the opening and of the book's interpretive frame.

## Method

The book's method is rule-ordered interpretation. It asks whether interpretive choices can be made predictable, consistent, economical, and falsifiable enough to validate some readings while excluding others.

Core methodological commitments:

- Begin with the Greek text before inherited theological explanations.
- Treat ambiguity as data, not as a problem to erase too quickly.
- Distinguish plausibility from justification.
- Identify ellipsis rather than silently supplying missing constituents.
- Prefer explanations that account for multiple phrases together over explanations that solve each phrase in isolation.
- Use intertextuality carefully and only after the local grammar and structure have been given full weight.
- Let the passage's own arrangement constrain interpretation.

## Core Technical Claims To Develop

- The verses have a surface/reduced/elliptical form and an underlying/expanded/non-elliptical form.
- The reduced form is the form present in the Greek editions.
- The passage contains seven core verbal heads, with `apokalypsis` treated as a special non-finite or gerund-like verbal head.
- `dei` and `genesthai` are important but are not counted among the seven core verbal heads because they belong to a modifying clause.
- All seven core phrases are elliptical.
- Six transmission phrases are arranged chiastically as A-B-C-C'-B'-A'.
- The chiasm begins at the lexical level and percolates upward to the phrasal level.
- The chiasm allows reinstatement of ellipted dependents.
- Reinstated constituents allow comparison and contrast among the phrase heads.
- The resulting structure changes the referents, agents, objects, and sequence often assumed by translators and commentators.

## Key Terms

- Ellipsis: the omission of a constituent that must be supplied for full sense.
- Surface form: the form of Revelation 1:1-3 as it appears in the Greek editions.
- Elliptical form: the same form considered as containing omitted dependents.
- Reduced form: the same form considered as compact and requiring expansion.
- Expanded form: the analytically reconstructed form with ellipted dependents made explicit.
- Non-elliptical form: the expanded form considered as no longer omitting required dependents.
- Underlying form: the structural relation beneath the surface arrangement.
- Core verbal head: a verbal head, including one verbal noun, that participates in forming a main non-modifying clause or clause-like unit.
- Core clause: a clause or clause-like unit headed by a core verbal head.
- Non-core verb: a verbal head that forms a modifying clause rather than one of the main clause-like units.
- Core phrase: one of the major phrases headed by a core verbal head in Revelation 1:1-3.
- Head: the central word or phrase governing a unit.
- Chiasm: an arrangement in which corresponding units mirror each other, often in an A-B-C-C'-B'-A' pattern.
- Signify: to communicate by signs, symbolic indication, or marked representation, not merely to state directly.

## Style Principles

- Explain every Greek or linguistic concept at the moment it becomes necessary.
- Avoid treating mainstream interpretations as foolish; show where they are plausible and why they are insufficient.
- Keep the reader oriented by distinguishing grammatical possibility from system-governed interpretation.
- Use diagrams when structure matters.
- Use short translated examples before technical discussion.
- Build from the opening phrase outward rather than dumping the whole theory at once.

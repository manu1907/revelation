# Chapter 11 Notes: The Servants

## Job Of The Chapter

Reassess `tois doulois autou` after the agency conclusions of Chapter 10.

## Core Argument

The servants are not simply all Christians generally. They are God's obedient recipients within the disclosure system. John is the named servant-witness; the plural servants are those to whom God shows and signifies the things that must happen. Later churches and readers are included insofar as they receive and keep the prophetic word as servants.

## Required Moves

- Treat the broad Christian reading as plausible because the book addresses churches and blesses readers/hearers/keepers.
- Show why `tois doulois autou` is structurally prior to the church address and reader/hearer blessing.
- Explain that `doulos` carries a master-servant frame of command, reception, loyalty, and obedience.
- Connect the plural servants to John as `to doulo autou Ioanne`.
- Test possible referents: prophetic servants, churches/readers, angelic servants.
- State the controlled conclusion without excluding later Christian readers.
- End by making `ha dei genesthai en tachei` the next necessary question.

## Avoid

- Do not claim the servants cannot include Christian readers.
- Do not flatten servants into a religious demographic.
- Do not isolate John from the servant category.
- Do not decide `en tachei`; save it for Chapter 12.

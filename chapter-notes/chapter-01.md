# Chapter 1 Notes: Why Revelation 1:1-3 Matters

## Job Of The Chapter

Open the book by showing that Revelation 1:1-3 is not a disposable preface. It determines how readers understand the sender, receiver, audience, timing, medium, content, and status of the book.

## Reader Problem

Most readers enter Revelation through inherited translations that sound straightforward:

- God gives something to Jesus Christ.
- Jesus Christ shows it to his servants.
- The events will happen soon.
- John writes the revelation.

The chapter should show that each of those statements contains interpretive decisions that the Greek does not make as simply as the English suggests.

## Core Argument

The opening verses are compressed. Their compression is not incidental. If the compression is ignored, translators and commentators must supply missing pieces. If those supplied pieces are not governed by a system, then the reading may become plausible but arbitrary.

## Key Scenes Or Examples

- A reader opening Revelation and being told from the first word what the book supposedly is.
- A comparison between a smooth English translation and a more literal rendering.
- A simple non-biblical example of ellipsis where supplied words change the meaning.

## Technical Concepts To Introduce

- Translation as interpretation.
- Ambiguity.
- Ellipsis.
- Rule-ordered interpretation.

## Avoid

- Do not begin with a dense Greek data dump.
- Do not attack commentators generally.
- Do not claim the whole consensus is wrong before showing why the issue matters.

## Draft Opening Possibility

The first word of Revelation may already have taught readers too much too quickly. In English, "The revelation of Jesus Christ" sounds familiar, settled, almost title-like. But in Greek the phrase is not a title printed on a cover. It is the beginning of a compressed sentence, and the decisions made there determine who gives, who receives, who shows, who signifies, who serves, what must happen, and when.

# Working Outline

## Part I: The Problem Hidden In The Opening

### Chapter 1: Why Revelation 1:1-3 Matters

Purpose: Show that the first three verses are not a preface to skip but the interpretive gateway to the whole book.

Core moves:

- Introduce the problem of familiar translations.
- Show how early decisions about agency, object, timing, and audience shape the reader's entire approach.
- Present the book's central question: can the interpretive choices in these verses be governed by a rule-ordered system rather than by plausibility alone?

### Chapter 2: The Trouble With Plausible Readings

Purpose: Explain why many expert interpretations are plausible but underdetermined.

Core moves:

- Discuss `apokalypsis iesou christou`.
- Discuss `hen edoken auto ho theos`.
- Discuss the subject of `deixai`.
- Discuss "his servants."
- Discuss `en tachei`.
- Introduce the problem of supplied words in `esemanen` and `aposteilas`.

### Chapter 3: What Counts As An Interpretation System?

Purpose: Define the standards the book will use.

Core moves:

- Explain rule-ordering, predictability, consistency, economy, and exclusion.
- Distinguish plausibility from constraint.
- Explain why a system can be useful without being perfectly symmetrical.
- Establish the methodological order: local grammar, ellipsis, structure, intertextuality, theology.

## Part II: The Greek As A Compressed Structure

### Chapter 4: The Reduced Form

Purpose: Describe the compact form on which translations and commentaries usually operate.

Core moves:

- Present Revelation 1:1-3 in Greek, transliteration, and a deliberately literal gloss.
- Identify the major translation decisions.
- Show where standard translations make the reduced form read smoothly.

### Chapter 5: Seven Core Phrases

Purpose: Identify the seven core verb-like heads or verbal phrases.

Core moves:

- Explain why `apokalypsis` is treated functionally as verbal.
- Explain why `dei genesthai` is important but not counted as a core phrase.
- List the seven core phrases.
- Show that each phrase is elliptical.

### Chapter 6: Ellipsis As Evidence

Purpose: Move ellipsis from a nuisance to the central evidence.

Core moves:

- Explain how ellipsis works in ordinary language and in Greek analysis.
- Show the ellipses in `esemanen` and `aposteilas`.
- Compare how experts supply missing constituents.
- Argue that supplying words must be governed by structure, not convenience.

### Chapter 7: The Chiasm

Purpose: Present the six-phrase chiastic arrangement.

Core moves:

- Lay out the proposed A-B-C-C'-B'-A' structure.
- Explain what each pair contributes.
- Show how the structure licenses reinstatement of ellipted constituents.
- Mark which parts of the case carry the most evidential weight.

### Chapter 8: The Expanded Form

Purpose: Present the non-elliptical reconstruction.

Core moves:

- Display the reduced and expanded forms side by side.
- Explain every supplied constituent.
- Show how the expanded form changes agent, object, audience, and sequence.

## Part III: Consequences For Meaning

### Chapter 9: What Is The "Apokalypsis"?

Purpose: Reassess the translation and referent of `apokalypsis`.

Core moves:

- Test "revelation," "disclosure," "unveiling," and related options.
- Argue why "revelation" misleads.
- Explain how the `apokalypsis` relates to, but does not equal, the book.

### Chapter 10: Who Receives What From Whom?

Purpose: Reassess `auto`, agency, and the chain of communication.

Core moves:

- Analyze the referent of `auto`.
- Reconsider whether Jesus Christ is the receiver.
- Argue for God as shower/signifier, if supported by the expanded form.
- Reassess whether Jesus Christ is the subject of `deixai`.

### Chapter 11: The Servants

Purpose: Reassess the audience implied by `tois doulois autou`.

Core moves:

- Show why "Christians generally" is an interpretive expansion.
- Examine possible referents and constraints.
- Connect the servants to the communication chain.

### Chapter 12: Soon, Speedily, Or In Speed?

Purpose: Reassess `en tachei`.

Core moves:

- Present the major options.
- Show what each option assumes about the events.
- Test the phrase inside the proposed structure.
- Explain whether the book requires imminence, manner, or another nuance.

### Chapter 13: Shown And Signified

Purpose: Reassess the relationship between showing and signifying.

Core moves:

- Distinguish showing from signifying.
- Argue that the things that must take place are both shown and signified, if the structure supports this.
- Connect this to the nature of John's visions and to multiple interpretations.

### Chapter 14: John's Testimony

Purpose: Clarify what John writes and what his testimony contains.

Core moves:

- Analyze the testimony to the word of God and testimony of Jesus Christ.
- Emphasize what John heard and saw.
- Contrast John's book with the entity miscalled "the revelation," if warranted.

## Part IV: Reading Revelation After The Opening

### Chapter 15: A Different Interpretive Frame

Purpose: Explain how the theory affects the reading of the rest of Revelation.

Core moves:

- Reconsider futurity.
- Reconsider novelty.
- Reconsider the expectation of linear sequence.
- Explain why multiple interpretations arise from the visionary mode.

### Chapter 16: Objections And Limits

Purpose: Address the strongest objections.

Core moves:

- Why not follow the near consensus?
- Is the reconstruction too complex?
- Does the chiasm force the evidence?
- What if `en tachei` still means "soon"?
- What parts of the theory are essential and what parts can be modified?

### Chapter 17: Conclusion

Purpose: State the result plainly.

Core moves:

- Summarize the interpretive system.
- Summarize the major changes in meaning.
- Invite rereading Revelation from the opening's own constraints.
